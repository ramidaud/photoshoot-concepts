Project Context

§ 01

The Challenge / Hypothesis

Oxytocin is widely known as the "bonding hormone" — the neurochemical of trust, love, and attachment in adults. But when does the oxytocin system first begin to shape the brain? Caldwell's work starts with a question most researchers never asked: what is oxytocin doing before birth?

The Solution / Innovation

Caldwell is the first researcher to systematically study oxytocin's role in prenatal brain development — specifically how oxytocin exposure in the womb shapes the developing neural architecture. Her findings have implications for understanding autism spectrum disorder, early attachment disorders, and the biological origins of social behavior. Her origin story is also the hook: she started with sea turtles. The same hormone that bonds a mother to her child was first studied in a reptile on a beach.

Behavioral and Developmental Correlation

Data connected to behavioral outcomes and developmental markers — the bridge between neurochemistry and the human story.

Posed / Environmental
Face and Discovery

Caldwell at the confocal microscope in a darkened room, her face lit only by the monitor showing fluorescence neural imagery — glowing receptor distribution patterns in the developing brain. This is a researcher illuminated by her own discovery. Same conceptual energy as the biofilm Concept A, adapted for neuroscience.

  • Full dark. Monitor is the only light source.
  • Position her so the screen glow spills onto her face and lab coat
  • Choose a microscopy frame with strong color separation — greens and blues ideal
  • Side profile or 3/4 — show both face and screen in the same frame
  • This is the one image that connects the scientist and the science without needing any other explanation

E
Documentary Portrait
The Science of Holding

The conceptual problem with the original prompts: The microscope face-glow portrait is strong but we already used that energy for the biofilm brief. The bigger idea here is that this is a story about connection — between a mother and child, between a turtle on a beach and a discovery about human attachment. The images should carry emotional warmth, not just scientific atmosphere.

The idea: A mother holds a newborn or infant — photographed in near darkness with Caldwell's fluorescence neural imagery projected across both of them. The oxytocin receptor distribution patterns in green and blue flow across the mother's face, the curve of her shoulder, and the child's head. The science of bonding literally illuminating the act of bonding. Caldwell is not in this image. The research is. This is the most direct possible statement of what the work means.

Environmental Documentary
The Origin

The idea: A sea turtle track crossing a dark beach at low tide, the parallel lines leading from the water's edge to a nest site out of frame. Shot at blue hour — dark sky, silver water, the sand almost black. The tracks are the only subject. No turtle, no researcher, no lab. Just the trace of the animal that started a question that led, unexpectedly, to understanding human attachment. The simplest possible image that carries the origin story.

Action Items

§ 04

Pre-Shoot — Research Coordination

Contact Caldwell's lab to schedule during an active microscopy session.
Request the most visually striking fluorescence microscopy frames in advance — review before shoot day.
Ask whether turtle imagery or specimens are accessible for Concept B.
Confirm whether her lab has confocal capability on-site or if a different facility is used.

Pre-Shoot — Technical Prep

Identify and test the darkened microscopy room before shoot day.
Bring f/1.4 prime — essential for Concept A (monitor-only light).
Test the monitor glow color temperature — adjust white balance accordingly.

Shoot Day

Start with Concept C (clean editorial portrait) to warm her up.
Schedule Concept A (microscope session) during actual imaging work.
Set up Concept D (monitor close-up) during the same microscopy session.
Concept B (turtle + brain juxtaposition) can be set up as a separate still life at the end.

Post-Shoot

Caldwell's Concept A (monitor portrait) is a strong UPAA candidate.
Coordinate final images with Field Notes editorial team. --- ---
