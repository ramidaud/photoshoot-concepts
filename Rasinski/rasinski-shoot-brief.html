Project Context

§ 01

The Challenge / Hypothesis

Reading fluency — the ability to read accurately, quickly, and with expression — is the bridge between decoding individual words and actually comprehending meaning. It is one of the most important and least-studied aspects of literacy development. For decades, fluency was overlooked in favor of phonics and comprehension. Rasinski asked what was being lost in the middle.

The Solution / Innovation

Rasinski's four decades of research produced the most widely used reading fluency frameworks in American education. His work is in millions of classrooms, shaping how teachers approach the quietest part of reading instruction. This is a legacy story — not a discovery story. The visual challenge is representing influence at scale: how do you photograph an idea that lives in the heads of children across the country? The answer is to photograph the human source of it, and the moment of transmission.

Shot Concepts

§ 03
A
Posed / Environmental
The Teacher

Rasinski in a classroom — either his university teaching space or a K-12 classroom with students reading. Warm, human, direct eye contact. The books, the space, the students (if present and permitted) fill the environment. This is a portrait of a man whose work has outlasted any single study — the portrait of influence.

  • Warm, soft light — this is a warmth-forward story, not a clinical one
  • If students are present: get releases, shoot from angles that respect their privacy, use them as environmental elements rather than subjects
  • If without students: a classroom of empty desks reads as equally powerful
  • Horizontal for editorial spread; vertical for profile

Documentary / Candid
The Moment of Reading

A child or student reading aloud — the fundamental act. A teacher or Rasinski listening, leaning in slightly. This is the image of what the research is for. Simple, warm, human.

  • If shooting in a real classroom, confirm all releases and permissions first
  • The reader's face should be visible — not obscured by the book
  • The listener's attention is the composition anchor
  • Window light or soft available light — don't overlight this scene
  • Horizontal preferred — the connection between reader and listener needs space

Forty Years

Rasinski in his office — surrounded by four decades of books, manuscripts, correspondence, and the accumulated material of a life spent thinking about how people read. He doesn't need to look at camera. This is the quiet image of legacy.

  • Warm, low key light to create depth and shadow
  • He can be reading, writing, looking out a window — not posed for the camera

E
Documentary / Telephoto
The View From Outside

The conceptual problem with the original prompts: A professor with a book in a classroom is a university brochure, not a Field Notes image. The research question here is about scale and transmission — how does one person's thinking reach millions of classrooms? The images should be about presence at a distance, influence without visibility.

The idea: An extreme telephoto shot (400mm equivalent) through a classroom window from outside the building. A child is reading aloud at a desk; a teacher listens nearby. Rasinski is not in the image. The window glass creates a slight softening, a slight remove. The camera watches from outside a room it cannot enter — exactly the position of someone whose influence has traveled further than their body. The image is about the rooms where his work lives without him.

F
Experimental Portrait
The Library at Night

The idea: A long-exposure deliberate double exposure. A library or reading room, shot twice from a tripod: once with Rasinski reading alone at a table under a pool of lamplight, once empty. The two exposures blended together produce a ghost — he is present and absent in the same image, solid enough to read by but transparent enough to see through. This is what legacy looks like: an impression left in a space after the person has gone.

Action Items

§ 04

Pre-Shoot — Research Coordination

Confirm whether classroom photography with students is feasible — secure all permissions before scheduling.
Ask Rasinski to identify a teacher currently using his methods who might be open to being photographed.
Request to see his office before the shoot — assess natural light and the density of materials.

Pre-Shoot — Technical Prep

85mm or 135mm essential for candid classroom work — keep distance.
Bring extra battery capacity for available-light shooting.
Identify the best window light in his office for Concept C.

Shoot Day

Concept C (office portrait) first — this is on his territory and warms the relationship.
Concept A (classroom portrait) second — in a space he's comfortable in.
Concept B (reading moment) if classroom access with students is cleared.
Concept D (book detail) can be done in 10 minutes at the end.

Post-Shoot

This story's visual strength is warmth and human scale — prioritize the images that feel most human over the most "impressive." --- ---
